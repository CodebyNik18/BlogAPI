The Art of Writing Simply and Clearly

February 27, 2026 10:30 AM

Good writing is like a windowpane. The best prose gets out of the way of the ideas it carries, letting meaning shine through without distraction or unnecessary ornamentation. This is harder than it sounds.

Most writers, when they start out, feel the urge to demonstrate their intelligence through complexity — long sentences, obscure vocabulary, elaborate structures. But the reader does not care how hard you worked. The reader cares only about the experience of reading, and a difficult experience is rarely a rewarding one.

The goal is clarity. Clarity of thought, clarity of structure, clarity of language. When you find yourself reaching for a complicated word, ask whether a simpler one would do. It almost always will.

Writing simply is not the same as writing badly. A clear sentence can carry enormous weight. A plain word can resonate for years. What we are after is not the absence of style but the presence of precision — saying exactly what you mean, no more, no less.

Start with the idea. Before you write a single word, know what you are trying to say. Not roughly, not approximately — exactly. If you cannot state your point in one clear sentence, you do not yet understand it well enough to write about it.
